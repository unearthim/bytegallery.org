The Original Sin

Before streaming, before MP3s, before anyone had heard of a "skip button," there was the MIDI file. A tiny, synthesized song file‚Äîoften under 50KB‚Äîthat would begin playing the moment a webpage loaded. No warning. No consent. Just instant, tinny music piped directly into your speakers at whatever volume you'd last set them to.

The MIDI (Musical Instrument Digital Interface) file wasn't inherently evil. It was a brilliant piece of technology from 1983, designed to let electronic instruments communicate. A MIDI file didn't store audio‚Äîit stored instructions: which notes to play, for how long, with what intensity. Your computer's sound card would interpret these instructions using its own synthesizer.

"The problem wasn't MIDI. The problem was autoplay. The problem was that every teenager with a Geocities page thought their homepage needed a soundtrack."

And because MIDI files were so small, they were perfect for the dial-up era. A full orchestral rendition of Beethoven's 5th could fit in 30KB. Compare that to a 3-minute MP3 at 3MB+, and you can see why webmasters reached for MIDI.

The Experience

Imagine: You're researching a school project. It's 11 PM. Your family is asleep. You click a link from a search engine (probably AltaVista or Yahoo). The page loads.

And then: MIDI rendition of "Fur Elise" at maximum volume.

You scramble for the volume knob. Too late. Your parents are awake. The dog is barking. The cat has fled to another dimension. And you can't find the stop button because it's hidden in a 2x2 pixel <embed> tag with hidden="true".

This wasn't a bug. This was intentional design. Webmasters wanted you to hear their curated soundtrack. They wanted ambiance. They wanted their homepage to feel like walking into a store with background music. What they created was closer to being trapped in an elevator with a malfunctioning sound system.

Historical Timeline

1983
MIDI standard is introduced for electronic instruments. Revolutionary for music production. Eventually becomes revolutionary for annoying website visitors.
1995-1996
Netscape Navigator and Internet Explorer add support for embedded media. The <embed> and <bgsound> tags are born. Pandora's box is opened.
1997-1999
Peak MIDI era. Geocities pages, fan sites, and personal homepages universally adopt background music. "MIDI archives" become popular destinations. The web becomes loud.
2000-2002
Broadband becomes more common. MP3 files start to replace MIDIs. Flash sites introduce better audio control. The tide begins to turn.
2003-2005
Web standards evolve. CSS and JavaScript offer better control. Autoplay becomes stigmatized. MIDI fades into memory, preserved only in retro homages and nightmares.
2017-Present
Major browsers block autoplay audio by default. Chrome, Firefox, and Safari require user interaction. The ghost of the MIDI file finally rests.

Cultural Impact

The embedded MIDI taught the web a hard lesson: Just because you can, doesn't mean you should.

It was the web's first major confrontation with the ethics of user agency. Before MIDI, websites were mostly passive‚Äîyou read them, you clicked links. MIDI made the web intrusive. It acted on you without asking.

This debate would repeat itself endlessly:

  • Auto-playing Flash intros (2000s)
  • Auto-playing video ads (2010s)
  • Push notifications (2010s-present)
  • Modal pop-ups and newsletter sign-ups (present)

The MIDI was patient zero for web annoyance. It showed us that technical possibility doesn't equal good UX. It taught designers that users want control.

"The MIDI didn't kill the personal homepage. But it certainly didn't help."

Why We Miss It (Kind Of)

Here's the strange part: despite the trauma, there's a weird fondness for MIDI culture. Not because the execution was good, but because it represented an era when the web was still expressive.

In 1999, your homepage was yours. You chose the MIDI. You chose the background tile. You chose the animated GIF of flames or stars. It was chaotic, it was garish, it was often borderline unusable‚Äîbut it was personal.

Today's web is cleaner, faster, more professional. But it's also more homogeneous. Every site uses the same frameworks, the same fonts, the same design patterns. The MIDI era was the wild west. The modern web is a gated community.

So when we hear those tinny, robotic renditions of classical music, we're not just hearing bad audio. We're hearing a time when anyone could make something weird and put it on the internet. And that's worth remembering.
